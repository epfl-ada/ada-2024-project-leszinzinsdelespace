Saving Christmas

Understanding User Frustration in Wikispeedia

Wikispeedia lets users chase connections between ideas - but sometimes, the path to victory vanishes unexpectedly. Players think they're close, only to hit dead-ends where crucial links are missing.

Imagine starting on "Bob Dylan," aiming for "Christmas," and wandering through "Winter," "Snow," and "Santa Claus" - only to find "Christmas" doesn't exist. The game reveals the fascinating (and frustrating) gaps in how we connect concepts.

The struggle to stay on track

Navigating Wikispeedia should feel intuitive and rewarding, yet many users encounter moments of frustration that disrupt their experience. Embedded within our dataset are subtle yet telling behaviors that collectively reveal the underlying struggles of users.

Frustration manifests not just in what users do, but in how they deviate from expected pathways. Backtracking, for instance, serves as a clear signalâ€”a tacit acknowledgment by users that the current route feels insufficient or misleading.

Figure 1: Relationship between game duration and number of backtracks.

If a game drags on too long, frustration can build as players wait longer for the rewarding sense of completion. As illustrated in the graph above, there is a noticeable correlation between the frequency of backtracking and overall game duration: each backtrack adds an average of 43 seconds to the game time.

Why the path feels broken

These recurring patterns of visibly frustrated user behavior stem from the fact that there seem to be unexpected dead-ends and unreachable targets. A simple analysis of the pages at which players backtrack most often shows that backtracking is associated with closely related concepts not being linked together in the hyperlink network.

Upon close inspection of certain paths, we notice that some target articles do not exist at all, forcing the user to repeatedly think they are very close to the target, only to find themselves having to backtrack.

If we take a closer look at the infamous "Bob Dylan" to "Christmas" game, we stumble upon something very interesting: the ups and downs in cosine similarity along the path reveal critical moments of coherence and disconnection. The fluctuations show that certain transitions between articles are semantically smooth, marked by high similarity scores, while others involve abrupt drops, indicating potential cognitive roadblocks for players.

Figure: Semantic similarity analysis of the path from "Bob Dylan" to "Christmas"

For instance, the sudden rise in similarity around "A Christmas Carol" suggests a clear connection to the target topic, providing players with a sense of progress. On the other hand, sharp dips, such as transitions to unrelated concepts like "Domesticated Turkey" or "Roman Britain," highlight moments where players might feel lost or misdirected. These valleys in similarity likely represent key frustration points, where the semantic bridge between concepts feels incomplete or unintuitive.

These patterns tell a story of how players navigate conceptual leaps, with peaks offering clear guidance and troughs reflecting moments of struggle.

Figure: Distribution of clicked links by position in article

Our analysis uncovers a fascinating pattern in the endgame stageâ€”even in unfinished paths. By zooming in on the highest similarity point within each game and the final eight moves leading up to it, we can piece together several intriguing scenarios for every Wikispeedia game:

  • Lost Player: The player explores validly but doesn't break a cosine similarity (CS) threshold of 0.3.
  • Frustrated Player: They reach a high CS (>0.3) but hit a dead-end due to missing links, blocking progress.
  • Unobservant Player: A key link exists (CS > 0.3) but is overlooked and not clicked. This hints at interface design improvements.
  • Player Messing Around: They navigate aimlessly, forming circular paths with no real progress, indicating data to filter or clean.
  • Victorious Player: They reach the target, offering insights into optimal navigation.

Tools to Light the Way

We've developed the Wikispeedia Missing Link Research Tool to enhance both player experience and network design.

Decision flow for link analysis

Our analysis tool categorizes article relationships into four key scenarios:

  • Linked: Direct connection exists
  • Not Linked (Appropriate): No connection needed
  • Missing Link: Should be connected
  • Non-existent: Article needs creation

Beyond the broken links

As an added bonus, we have created the Wikispeedia Missing Link Research Tool, a resource designed to enhance both player experience and network design. By identifying potential missing links and areas of frustration, this tool provides actionable insights for improving the hyperlink network - and saving Christmas by letting Santa get to Christmas.

About This Project

This website presents the findings of our Applied Data Analysis (CS-401) project at EPFL. Using data from Wikispeedia, we analyzed how players navigate between Wikipedia articles, focusing on understanding patterns of user frustration and identifying missing semantic connections.
