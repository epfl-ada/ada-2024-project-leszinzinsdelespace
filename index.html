Saving Christmas

Understanding User Frustration in Wikispeedia

Imagine trying to get to "Christmas" in Wikispeedia, only to find yourself stuck at "Winter" or "Santa Claus" with no way forward. Frustrating, isn't it? That is precisely the situation a player found themselves in. But this isn't just about one frustrated player's holiday quest - it's a window into a broader pattern of disconnection.

In Wikispeedia, players chase connections between ideas, but sometimes these paths vanish unexpectedly. What starts as an engaging puzzle can become a dead end, revealing gaps in our digital knowledge network that mirror gaps in how we connect concepts.

The Ups and Downs of Wikispeedia

The struggle to stay on track

Navigating Wikispeedia should feel intuitive and rewarding, yet many users encounter moments of frustration that disrupt their experience. Embedded within our dataset are subtle yet telling behaviors that collectively reveal the underlying struggles of users.

The Human Side of Digital Frustration

Frustration in Wikispeedia isn't just about failed navigationâ€”it's a deeply human experience. When players start their journey, they're driven by anticipation and the promise of reward, their brains ready for the satisfaction of finding the right path. But as they encounter dead ends and missing links, this optimism transforms into a cocktail of stress hormones.

They begin with excitement, dopamine driving their pursuit. As they navigate through "Winter" and reach "Santa Claus," initial confidence turns to confusion. When the expected link to "Christmas" isn't there, cortisol levels rise, and adrenaline kicks inâ€”the body's natural "fight or flight" response to an increasingly frustrating situation. What started as an engaging puzzle becomes a source of genuine emotional stress, leading many players to eventually abandon their quest.

Frustration manifests not just in what users do, but in how they deviate from expected pathways. Backtracking, for instance, serves as a clear signalâ€”a tacit acknowledgment by users that the current route feels insufficient or misleading.

Why the path feels broken

These recurring patterns of visibly frustrated user behavior stem from the fact that there seem to be unexpected dead-ends and unreachable targets. A simple analysis of the pages at which players backtrack most often shows that backtracking is associated with closely related concepts not being linked together in the hyperlink network.

Upon close inspection of certain paths, we notice that some target articles do not exist at all, forcing the user to repeatedly think they are very close to the target, only to find themselves having to backtrack.

For example, the most frequent backtrack in our dataset is from "Soybean" when "Bean" is the target, with 111 recorded backtracks.

These patterns of frustration aren't random - they point to something deeper in how our minds connect ideas versus how Wikispeedia's network is structured. To understand this disconnect, we need to look beyond simple link counting and into the semantic relationships between articles.

We thought it might be interesting to compute the cosine similarity for every position in the path of every game. If we take a closer look at the infamous "Bob Dylan" to "Christmas" game, we stumble upon something very interesting: the ups and downs in cosine similarity along the path reveal critical moments of coherence and disconnection. The fluctuations show that certain transitions between articles are semantically smooth, marked by high similarity scores, while others involve abrupt drops, indicating potential cognitive roadblocks for players.

We evaluated several embedding models (BERT, GloVe, and OpenAI) on this specific path. OpenAI's embeddings proved superior by:

  • Capturing nuanced relationships between conceptually related articles (e.g., "Winter" â†’ "Christmas")
  • Providing more consistent similarity scores across different semantic domains
  • Better reflecting human intuition about concept relationships

For instance, the sudden rise in similarity around "A Christmas Carol" suggests a clear connection to the target topic, providing players with a sense of progress. On the other hand, sharp dips, such as transitions to unrelated concepts like "Domesticated Turkey" or "Roman Britain," highlight moments where players might feel lost or misdirected. These valleys in similarity likely represent key frustration points, where the semantic bridge between concepts feels incomplete or unintuitive.

These patterns tell us a story: a story of how players navigate conceptual leaps, with peaks offering clear guidance and troughs reflecting moments of struggle.

By looking at the different types of game paths, we can classify each game into one of four categories:

  • Lost Player: The player explores validly but doesn't break a cosine similarity (CS) threshold of 0.3.
  • Frustrated Player: They reach a high CS (>0.3) but hit a dead-end due to missing links, blocking progress.
  • Unobservant Player: A key link exists (CS > 0.3) but is overlooked and not clicked. This hints at interface design improvements.
  • Victorious Player: They reach the target, offering insights into optimal navigation.

Fixing missing links

In Wikispeedia's network of articles, not every page is reachable from every other page. This fundamental connectivity issue means that some games are impossible to complete from the start.

Initial state of Wikispeedia's network

The large circle (4,051 articles) represents interconnected pages, while smaller circles show isolated articles that can't be reached from the main network, making some games impossible to complete.

As we can see, there is a big cluster containing most of the articles that can be mutually reached. But there is still more than 500 articles (around 12%) that are either not reachable from any other article or that can't reach most articles.

Our goal is to make every article reachable from any starting point, enhancing the game's playability while preserving its core challenge. We'll focus on adding only the most essential connections - those that users naturally expect to find - rather than artificially flooding the network with links.

Let's revisit our table of most frequent backtracks. The most common frustration point occurs between "Soybean" and "Bean", with 111 recorded backtracks. This suggests a missing connection that should be obvious to users.

Looking at the actual content of the Soybean article, we find something interesting:

"...Beans are classed as pulses whereas soybeans are classed as oilseeds. The word soy is derived from the Japanese word shoyu (soy sauce/soya sauce)..."

The word "bean" explicitly appears in the article, yet there's no hyperlink to the Bean page! This is a clear example of a missing semantic connection - the link should exist but was simply overlooked during the network's creation. Such oversights, while small, may significantly impact user experience and navigation patterns.

Link Addition Criteria

Using this insight, we developed a systematic approach: add a link between articles when:

  1. The name of one article appears in the content of another, and
  2. The two articles have a strong semantic similarity (cosine similarity > 0.5)

This approach ensures we only add links that are both contextually relevant and semantically meaningful.

Network connectivity after adding semantic links

After adding links based on semantic similarity, the main connected component grew to 4,100 articles. While this is an improvement, it shows that semantic similarity alone isn't enough to fully connect the network.

The graph above visualizes the network structure after implementing our semantic similarity-based link additions.

While this improvement is modest - only 49 additional articles became reachable - it confirms our hypothesis that some disconnected articles are semantically related and referenced within each other. However, the small increase also suggests that we need additional strategies to significantly improve the network's connectivity.

We then tried a less restrictive approach: add a link whenever one article's name appears in another's content, regardless of semantic similarity.

Network connectivity with all possible in-article links

Adding all possible in-article links creates an overwhelmingly connected network. While only 17 articles remain isolated, the addition of 372,813 new links would make the game too easy and lose its educational value.

This approach dramatically improved connectivity, but at a severe cost: it added 372,813 new linksâ€”more than quintupling the total number of connections in the network. Such a dense web of links would fundamentally change the nature of Wikispeedia, making it too easy to find connections and removing the creative challenge that makes the game engaging.

This reveals an important tension in our mission: while we want to make the game more accessible by eliminating impossible paths, we must be careful not to sacrifice the core gameplay experience that makes Wikispeedia both challenging and rewarding.

But is it even possible to make every article reachable from any starting point? Maybe there is an article that is so specific that it would make no sense to even try to connect it to other articles. To elucidate this, we decided to plot the embeddings of all articles (by reducing the dimensionality of the embeddings to 2 dimensions using t-SNE) and see if there are any completely isolated articles, especially words that are not in the main cluster.

Figure: t-SNE visualization of article embeddings showing connected (blue) and isolated (red) articles in semantic space.

Interestingly, the visualization reveals that there are no truly semantically isolated articles - even the currently disconnected articles (shown in red) are semantically similar to some connected articles (in blue), as evidenced by their proximity in the semantic space. This suggests that the current disconnections in the network are more a result of missing links rather than fundamental semantic incompatibility between articles.

To be able to connect all articles, we devised an algoorithm that would, at each step, add a link between the isolated article that is the most similar to any article in the main cluster and this article of the main cluster. So the selected article (and articles connected to it) would now be part of the main cluster. We repeated this process until all articles were connected.

Figure: t-SNE visualization of links added by our algorithm. They may not seem optimal since similarity in the reduced space is not necessarily the same as similarity in the original (3072-D) space.

Now this is the same semantic space with our algorithm's proposed connections overlaid in red. These new links bridge semantic gaps while maintaining meaningful relationships between articles, strategically connecting isolated nodes to the main network without overwhelming it with unnecessary connections.

By carefully balancing semantic similarity and network structure, we developed an optimized approach that achieves full connectivity while preserving the game's challenge.

Final optimized network connectivity

Our optimized approach successfully connected all 4,604 articles with just 525 carefully chosen new links. This achieves full connectivity while preserving the game's challenge, creating a balance between playability and difficulty.

The visualization above represents a remarkable achievement: we've successfully connected all 4,604 articles into a single strongly connected component. This means every article is now reachable from any other article, creating a fully navigable network. What's particularly impressive is that we achieved this with only 525 carefully chosen additional links - a mere fraction of the potential connections we could have added.

The single green circle in the graph, labeled "4604", represents this unified network where all articles are mutually accessible. This is in stark contrast to the initial fragmented state we saw earlier, where many articles were isolated or part of disconnected components. Our selective approach to adding links has maintained the game's challenge while eliminating impossible paths.

It's important to note that these limitations in connectivity are partly due to the nature of the dataset itself.
