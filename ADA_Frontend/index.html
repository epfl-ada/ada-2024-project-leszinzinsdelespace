Understanding User Frustration in Wikispeedia

Wikispeedia lets users chase connections between ideas - but sometimes, the path to victory vanishes unexpectedly. Players think they're close, only to hit dead-ends where crucial links are missing.

Imagine starting on "Bob Dylan," aiming for "Christmas," and wandering through "Winter," "Snow," and "Santa Claus" - only to find "Christmas" doesn't exist. The game reveals the fascinating (and frustrating) gaps in how we connect concepts.

The struggle to stay on track

Why the path feels broken

These recurring patterns of visibly frustrated user behavior stem from the fact that there seem to be unexpected dead-ends and unreachable targets. Upon close inspection of certain paths, we notice that some target articles do not exist at all, forcing the user to repeatedly think they are very close to the target, only to find themselves having to backtrack.

In a similar fashion, users very often follow their intuition to a pages that logically would link to their target article (why wouldn't "Soybean" link to "Bean"?) but discover that the desired link is either completely missing from the page or only present as text (no hyperlink), which is perhaps even more frustrating.

But this, while infuriating for users, leads to a very intriguing question:

How would one go about finding the missing semantic links that lead to repeated and consistent user frustration?

Reconnecting the dots

This is where the notion of semantic distance comes in.

Semantic distance is a quantitative measure of the conceptual difference between two entities in a semantic space. In the context of Wikispeedia, it is computed using the complement of cosine similarity between the vector representations of two articles.
