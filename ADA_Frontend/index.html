Understanding User Frustration in Wikispeedia

Wikispeedia lets users chase connections between ideas - but sometimes, the path to victory vanishes unexpectedly. Players think they're close, only to hit dead-ends where crucial links are missing.

The struggle to stay on track

Navigating Wikispeedia should feel intuitive and rewarding, yet many users encounter moments of frustration that disrupt their experience. Embedded within our dataset are subtle yet telling behaviors that collectively reveal the underlying struggles of users. Frustration manifests not just in what users do, but in how they deviate from expected pathways. Backtracking, for instance, serves as a clear signal‚Äîa tacit acknowledgment by users that the current route feels insufficient or misleading.

These obstacles challenge players' perseverance and highlight gaps in the network structure.

If a game drags on too long, frustration can build as players wait longer for the rewarding sense of completion. As illustrated in the graph above, there is a noticeable correlation between the frequency of backtracking and overall game duration: each backtrack adds an average of 43 seconds to the game time.

Backtracking is a clear sign of frustration and a roadblock for the players - the more a player backtracks, the more difficult he perceives the game to be.

Similarly, unfinished games become a reflection of deeper systemic issues: moments when the cognitive cost of continuing outweighs the motivation to persist. Even more revealing are the repeated encounters with "dead ends"‚Äîarticles devoid of meaningful connections. These moments highlight the fragility of a system that relies on semantic coherence, pointing to gaps where structure and intuition fail to meet. In examining these behaviors, we move beyond isolated actions to uncover a narrative of user experience, one where frustration is not just an obstacle but a window into the complexities of human navigation and decision-making. Frustration is an emotion that we all experience as human beings in our daily lives: when we fail a tricky maths exercise or the queue at the bakery is too long: it arises when efforts to achieve something come up against repeated obstacles or failures, causing a feeling of tension and dissatisfaction. Playing a game like wikispeedia with inaccessible pages can lead to a cocktail of hormones in our users, resulting in growing frustration and abandonment in some of our players. An increase in cortisol, adrenalin, and a drop in dopamine are all factors in the abandonment of our wikispeedia players who were waiting to find the Christmas page. Let's follow our player on his emotional journey: when he starts playing, he's driven by his search for **dopamine** and his reward circuit: he wants to find page Santa as quickly as possible to feel good and satisfied. As the game progresses, our player realizes that he can't find the target page: the **dopamine** he's looking for becomes increasingly inaccessible and diminishes, increasing his sense of frustration. As the player moves forward and gets stuck between the Santa page and the Christmas page, frustration and **stress levels** rise, and the body releases **cortisol** and a dose of **adrenalin**. Physiologically, an increase in these hormones creates emotional difficulties for our player: this is the beginning of the **‚Äúfight or flight‚Äù** response for our player, who will get angry trying to find a link between our two pages, and who will eventually give up and blame Santa.

Why the path feels broken

These recurring patterns of visibly frustrated user behavior stem from the fact that there seem to be unexpected dead-ends and unreachable targets. A simple analysis of the pages at which players backtrack most often shows that backtracking is associated with closely related concepts not being linked together in the hyperlink network. Upon close inspection of certain paths, we notice that some target articles do not exist at all, forcing the user to repeatedly think they are very close to the target, only to find themselves having to backtrack.

Reconnecting the dots

This is where the notion of semantic distance comes in.

Semantic distance is a quantitative measure of the conceptual difference between two entities in a semantic space. In the context of Wikispeedia, it is computed using the complement of cosine similarity between the vector representations of two articles.
